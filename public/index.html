Press Live 07:00 Asia / Singapore

At precisely seven in the morning, Singapore time, an unseen press stirs to life inside a server in another hemisphere. It opens the previous day's ledger of new signups, files them in order of importance, and dispatches the result to a single inbox before its owner has reached for the kettle.

The Morning Briefing is not read by anyone but its commissioner. It does not court subscribers. It exists for the small, quiet pleasure of waking to a desk already cleared — and for the durable comfort of work that happens whether one remembers it or not.

The Process

  1. I.

    The Cron Strikes Seven

    Vercel's scheduler fires at 23:00 UTC — the precise moment the clock turns 07:00 in Singapore. An HTTP request is made, in confidence, to the briefing endpoint, authenticated by a bearer secret the world does not see.

  2. II.

    The Ledger Is Opened

    A query is dispatched to Supabase asking only for signups that occurred between yesterday's first minute and yesterday's last — measured in the reader's own timezone, not the server's. The window is correct even when the world's clocks disagree.

  3. III.

    The Briefing Is Drafted

    The rows are handed to Groq's llama-3.3-70b, with instructions to compose an opening line, a breakdown by cohort, a ranked list of who to reach first, and a suggested opener for the most promising name.

  4. IV.

    The Edition Goes Out

    Resend delivers the primary copy. Gmail follows as a faithful understudy. Both inboxes report receipt. The press goes quiet until the following dawn — and any morning the table is bare, it says so plainly.
