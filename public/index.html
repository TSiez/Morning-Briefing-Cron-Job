Filed Daily at 07:00 Singapore · One Reader · No Advertising, Ever

At precisely seven in the morning, Singapore time, an unseen press stirs to life inside a server in another hemisphere. It opens the previous day's ledger of new signups, files them in order of importance, and dispatches the result to a single inbox before its owner has reached for the kettle.

The Morning Briefing is not read by anyone but its commissioner. It does not court subscribers. It exists for the small, quiet pleasure of waking to a desk already cleared — and for the durable comfort of work that happens whether one remembers it or not.

The Process

How each morning's edition goes to press

No. I

The Cron Strikes Seven

Vercel's scheduler fires at 23:00 UTC — the moment the clock turns seven in Singapore — and calls the briefing endpoint in confidence, behind a bearer secret.

No. II

The Ledger Is Opened

Supabase is asked only for signups between yesterday's first and last minute, measured in the reader's own timezone — correct even when the world's clocks disagree.

No. III

The Brief Is Drafted

The rows go to Groq's llama-3.3-70b, which writes an opening line, a breakdown by cohort, a ranked list, and an opener for the most promising name.

No. IV

The Edition Goes Out

Resend delivers the copy, with Gmail as a faithful understudy. Both report receipt, and the press falls quiet until the following dawn.
